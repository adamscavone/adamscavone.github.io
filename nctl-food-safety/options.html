Option 1: Analytical method

How is allergen content actually measured? Three credible methods exist; each has a different cost/regulatory profile.

Dimension ELISA LC-MS/MS PCR (DNA)
What it measures Allergen protein (antibody binding) Allergen protein (peptide fragments) Allergen DNA presence/absence
Regulatory standing for FDA gluten-free claim AOAC 2012.01 official method (R5 Mendez) AOAC 2017.17 — First Action only, 4 allergens, limited matrices Not regulatory standard for <20 ppm threshold
Throughput per analyst-day ~80 samples (96-well plate) ~10–20 samples (depending on prep) ~30–50 samples
Reagent cost / sample $15–30 $5–15 + standards $8–15
Capex to launch $15–35K $0 (NCTL has LC-MS) + method dev cost $30–60K (thermocycler)
Time to first commercial result 6–10 weeks (per allergen) 6–12 months (per allergen-matrix pair) 3–6 months
Hydrolyzed / fermented matrix limitation Yes — antibodies miss fragmented protein Better, but FDA defers to manufacturer records for these foods regardless Detects DNA fragments OK
Multiplexable (one workflow, many allergens) One allergen per plate; one workflow per kit Yes — multi-allergen panels possible Yes

Regulatory note

FDA’s August 2020 final rule on gluten-free labeling for fermented and hydrolyzed foods states that gluten cannot currently be adequately detected and quantified for compliance purposes in those products. FDA uses manufacturer records (control programs, supplier verification) rather than test results. This means even LC-MS/MS doesn’t solve the regulatory problem in soy sauce / beer / hydrolyzed wheat protein matrices — both methods fall back to records review.

Peer LLM disagreement on this one

The two outside LLMs split on phasing. Gemini argued for Big 9 day-one (“food manufacturers refuse to split samples between labs; partial coverage cedes the account to Eurofins”). Codex argued for gluten+peanut MVP (“validation burden multiplies before you know which allergens produce paid demand”). Both arguments are defensible; the call depends on whether you expect cannabis-edibles customers to behave like food customers (refuse-to-split) or treat allergen testing as a la carte add-ons.
