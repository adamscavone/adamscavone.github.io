Recommendation

NCTL should stand up a “Food Safety Services” line with allergen testing (ELISA) plus on-site allergen-control swab programs, starting with a gluten + peanut MVP for cannabis-edibles producers in OH + MI, with cannabis-product authenticity queued as a year-2 adjacency.

Three reasons

  1. The launch is cheap and fast. ELISA on R-Biopharm RIDASCREEN gets to commercial-grade results in ~10 weeks at ~$25K capex. AOAC 2012.01 is the FDA-recognized safe harbor for the gluten-free <20 ppm labeling claim. One microplate reader runs every allergen kit — same instrument scales to the rest of the Big 9 when demand pulls.
  2. Bundling on-site swab programs converts testing into recurring revenue. Sample-in/results-out alone is $2-8K ARR per customer; adding on-site allergen-control verification (Hygiena AllerSnap / Neogen Reveal 3-D) pushes that to $15-30K ARR — and the geographic moat (local field service in OH + MI) locks out distant national competitors like Eurofins and Bia who can’t cost-effectively send technicians.
  3. Cannabis-product authenticity is a real adjacency on instruments NCTL already owns. The LC-MS/MS and GC-MS infrastructure can drive distillate composition, synthetic-terpene detection, and Delta-8 / HHC synthesis fingerprinting — positioned as “product-integrity analytics” for brands, not regulatory adjudication. Existing customer access, no incumbent, premium pricing. Year 2 sequence.

Three risks

  1. Validated customer demand has not been surveyed. The strategic case is conviction-based; if you want a 60-day customer-pull survey before committing capex, it’s defensible.
  2. FDA’s August 2020 rule explicitly says gluten in fermented / hydrolyzed foods cannot be reliably tested; FDA uses manufacturer records instead. NCTL must explicitly exclude those products from scope or sell them as records-review engagements, not routine compliance testing.
  3. The MVP-vs-Big-9 phasing call is genuinely contested. The MVP path (recommended) has lower validation overhead but higher sample-splitting risk (customers send their other-allergen work to Eurofins). The Big-9 path has higher launch friction but locks in full-panel positioning.

What changed our framing

The original prompt was “gluten test for our customers.” During scoping, three expansions changed the strategic shape:

  • Multi-allergen, not just gluten. The FDA Big 9 (milk, eggs, fish, crustacean, tree nuts, peanut, wheat / gluten, soy, sesame) are all addressable on the same microplate reader with kit swaps. Same workflow, same analyst, same capex. Peanut alone matters because cannabis-infused granolas and baked goods are exactly the cross-contamination matrix peanut allergies care about.
  • On-site services, not just testing. The food-safety regulatory stack (cGMP under 21 CFR Part 117, HACCP, FSMA preventive controls, allergen control programs, environmental monitoring) is buyable services. Per-customer ARR jumps from $2-8K to $15-30K when testing is bundled with on-site verification.
  • Food fraud is an adjacent revenue line, mostly on instruments NCTL already owns. Olive oil, honey, maple syrup, vanilla, and spice authenticity are LC-MS / GC-MS workflows. Cannabis-product authenticity (terpene authenticity, distillate composition, Delta-8 synthesis fingerprinting) is the same instruments applied to NCTL’s existing customer base — the highest-leverage entry because the customer relationships already exist and there’s no incumbent.

What is ELISA?

ELISA — Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay — is the regulatory-standard method for allergen detection. It uses antibodies that specifically bind the allergen protein. For gluten, the antibody is R5 Mendez (binds gliadin / secalin / hordein epitopes in wheat, rye, and barley). The format is a 96-well microplate sandwich:

Step 1. Plate well coated with capture antibody. Step 2. Sample extract added. Allergen (if present) binds the antibody. Step 3. Detection antibody (enzyme-conjugated) added. Forms a sandwich. Step 4. Substrate added. Enzyme produces a color change. Step 5. Plate reader measures absorbance at ~450 nm. [---R5---] <- capture antibody, bound to well | [Gluten protein] <- analyte from sample | [---R5---HRP] <- detection antibody + enzyme | [color] <- intensity proportional to gluten concentration

Why ELISA wins for the launch:

  • It’s the AOAC official method. AOAC 2012.01 (R-Biopharm RIDASCREEN Gliadin) is the Codex Type I R5 method and the safe-harbor for FDA gluten-free <20 ppm labeling claims. LC-MS/MS exists (AOAC 2017.17) but it’s First Action status only, covers four allergens in limited matrices, and requires labeled peptide internal standards plus matrix-matched validation per product.
  • It’s fast. ~80 samples per plate, ~2.5 hours hands-on + incubation. Reagent cost $15-30 per sample. One analyst, one bench.
  • It’s sensitive enough. Quantitation range 5-80 ppm; detection limit ~3-5 ppm. The FDA threshold is <20 ppm. ELISA sits squarely in that range.
  • It scales. Same plate reader runs every Big 9 allergen kit. Capex doesn’t multiply when you add allergens to the panel.

The one place ELISA loses

Hydrolyzed and fermented matrices — soy sauce, beer, hydrolyzed wheat protein — chop up the gluten epitopes so the R5 antibody can’t bind reliably. The result is false negatives. But LC-MS/MS doesn’t solve this either. FDA’s August 12, 2020 final rule on gluten-free labeling for fermented and hydrolyzed foods explicitly says gluten cannot currently be adequately detected or quantified in these products — FDA uses manufacturer records (control programs, supplier verification) rather than test results. The right scope decision: don’t accept fermented / hydrolyzed gluten-claim work as routine testing. Sell it as a records-review or custom R&D engagement, or refer it out.

Allergen panel scope

The FDA “Big 9” major food allergens (FALCPA + FASTER Act 2023):

  1. Milk
  2. Eggs
  3. Fish
  4. Crustacean shellfish
  5. Tree nuts
  6. Peanuts
  7. Wheat (gluten = wheat + barley + rye storage proteins)
  8. Soybeans
  9. Sesame (added Jan 1, 2023)

All 9 are addressable on the same plate reader with kit swaps. The economic question is not “should we add peanut?” — it’s “how broad is the panel at launch?”

Recommendation: gluten + peanut MVP at launch. Gluten gets the FDA-claim hook for cannabis-edibles labeling. Peanut covers the highest-severity cross-contact scenario for granolas, baked snacks, and shared-equipment edible production. Validating two allergens takes ~4-6 weeks vs. ~2 months for Big 9 parallel validation. Add milk and soy at month 4-6 if customer pull shows up; add the rest based on demand signal.

Why cannabis-product authenticity, not olive oil, is the entry point

Conventional food authenticity is the “safer” market but the worse entry for NCTL because the customer relationships don’t exist and the gold-standard methods (honey IRMS, vanilla isotope ratios) live on instruments NCTL doesn’t own. Cannabis-product authenticity is the opposite: nascent market, no incumbent, but the customers and instruments are already there. Peer-reviewed work has found Delta-8 products with impurities well beyond what COAs declared, and NIJ-funded LC-MS/MS work on commercial cannabinoid products documented systematic mislabeling. The sales motion is “product-integrity analytics” for brands that want defensible differentiation — not regulator-style adjudication, which the category isn’t mature enough to deliver yet.

What two outside reviewers said

We consulted Google Gemini and OpenAI Codex independently with the same four-question prompt. Both are LLMs with broad food-industry knowledge, not domain experts — treat their answers as triangulation, not gospel. They agreed on three of four calls and split on phasing.

Gemini (Google)

Method

Launch with ELISA. LC-MS lacks the cross-laboratory standardization for FDA gluten-free “safe harbor.” ELISA = $<15K equipment, near-instant launch; LC-MS allergen method validation = 6-9 months on instruments currently generating cannabinoid revenue.

Business model

Strategy B — testing + on-site swab programs. Transforms commodity testing into a sticky SLA aligned with FSMA. Locks out distant national competitors via local field service. Scales to $20K+ ARR per customer.

Phasing

Big 9 day-one. Food manufacturers refuse to split samples between labs — partial coverage cedes accounts to Eurofins or Mérieux. Defer food-fraud / authenticity to year 2.

Authenticity beachhead

Cannabis-product authenticity first — existing customer channel, no incumbent. Conventional food would require multi-year reputation-building against established reference labs.

Risk: cannabis authenticity is “marketing-driven discretionary spend” rather than compliance, so demand is brand-protection driven not regulatory.

Codex (OpenAI)

Method

ELISA as primary platform; LC-MS as a later exception build. AOAC 2017.17 (LC-MS allergen) is First Action only, 4 allergens, limited matrices, requires labeled peptide internal standards. ELISA = low-five-figure launch, 6-10 week validation; LC-MS build = 6-12 months.

Business model

Strategy B — testing + on-site swab programs. With ~50 addressable customers, pure samples-in/results-out is too thin. Use Neogen Reveal 3-D / Hygiena AllerSnap on-site. D is the wrong starting move — combines two pivots at once (consulting + authenticity) where some authenticity methods (honey IRMS) require instruments NCTL doesn’t own.

Phasing

Gluten + peanut MVP. Big 9 day-one multiplies validation burden, kit inventory, QC upkeep, and training before NCTL knows which allergens produce paid demand. Sequence: gluten + peanut → milk + soy on demand → sesame later. Authenticity year 2.

Authenticity beachhead

Cannabis-product authenticity first — commercially adjacent, instruments already owned. Peer-reviewed work has found Delta-8 products with impurities well beyond declared COAs; NIJ LC-MS/MS work documented systematic cannabinoid product mislabeling.

Risk: cannabis authenticity must be sold as “premium QA / brand protection,” not regulator-style bright-line adjudication — the category is too method-fragmented for that.

Where they agree

Both LLMs landed on ELISA + Strategy B + cannabis-product authenticity as the beachhead. The agreement isn’t the same as proof, but it’s real signal — two independent reads converging on the same three calls despite different reasoning paths.

Where they split — and how we’d call it

Gemini argues Big 9 day-one (refuse-to-split customer behavior). Codex argues gluten + peanut MVP (validation burden multiplies before demand signal exists). Our call: lean toward Codex’s MVP path. Gemini’s “refuse-to-split” argument is strong for established food-lab customers (CPG branded foods at Eurofins / Bia) but weaker for cannabis-edibles producers, who don’t today send the work anywhere and will likely treat allergen tests as a la carte add-ons. The MVP minimizes launch risk; expansion to Big 9 can be triggered by demand pull within 4-6 months. That said, this is the call where the exec should override us if domain instinct says “cannabis customers will demand full panel.”

Phasing — 90 / 180 / 365-day milestones

Days 0–90

  • Customer-demand survey: 5-10 conversations with active edibles producers in OH + MI. Cost: time only. Question to validate: would they pay $200–400 for gluten + peanut testing today, and would they engage NCTL for on-site sanitation work?
  • Capex sign-off and procurement: plate reader, washer, shaker, homogenizer, pipettes. Lead times 4-6 weeks.
  • Identify and contract the PCQI consultant for the on-site swab program. Start at hourly engagement, evaluate FTE at month 9.
  • Decide on branding (sub-brand / LLC / scope expansion) — affects website, customer communications, insurance.

Days 90–180

  • Gluten method validation: 4-6 weeks. AOAC 2012.01, R-Biopharm RIDASCREEN Gliadin. Validate on 3+ cannabis-edible matrices (gummy, baked good, beverage).
  • Peanut method validation: parallel, 4-6 weeks. RIDASCREEN Peanut.
  • Field protocol for allergen swab programs: SOPs, trending dashboard, corrective-action reporting template. (Codex’s caveat is right: sold as one-off site visits, this work decays to low-margin services; sold as ongoing programs with trending, it sticks.)
  • First commercial launch: gluten testing live by month 5-6. Peanut testing live by month 6.
  • ISO 17025 scope-expansion application: filed; takes 6-12 months to complete.

Days 180–365

  • Allergen panel expansion: milk + soy if demand pulls (month 7-9). Tree nut + egg if pull continues (month 10-12).
  • On-site swab program scales: target 5-8 active customer programs by month 12.
  • Cannabis-product authenticity method development begins (year 2 launch): synthetic-terpene detection, Delta-8 impurity fingerprinting. Use existing LC-MS / GC-MS. Method dev ~3-6 months in parallel with allergen scale-up.
  • FTE decision: convert PCQI consultant to payroll if utilization > 60%; stay contract otherwise.
  • Year-2 plan locks in: Tier C (full consulting) and / or conventional food authenticity if the core offer is repeatable and there’s capacity to take on a second business model.

What this is not

  • Not a market study. The instrument and cost ranges come from industry benchmarks and the two LLM consults; the $X ARR per customer estimates are illustrative. Customer-pull validation is decision 7 below and should precede capex.
  • Not a commitment to Tier C or D. The recommendation is explicitly Tier B (testing + swabs) for year 1. Full consulting and conventional food authenticity are deferred until the core offer is repeatable.
  • Not a partnership-vs-build verdict. The 60-day customer survey can also surface whether referring to Bia / Eurofins for 6 months is the right interim move while validation runs.
